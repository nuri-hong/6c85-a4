Proposition. Restrictive abortion laws shift abortions out of state rather than reducing them.

Preview Notes

The map shows a clear policy split by region. Restrictive policies cluster in the South, while protective policies cluster on the West Coast and in the Northeast. This pattern motivates the main question of the analysis, whether different legal environments are associated with cross state travel and abortion rates. I grouped states using this policy map, while noting a timing mismatch between 2026 policy data and 2020 abortion data. That mismatch limits inference, but the grouping still provides a useful framework for comparison.

FOR the Proposition: Restrictions do not eliminate abortions. They displace them.

Design decisions and rationale

  • Group policies into three categories: Restrictive, Some restrictions-protections, and Protective. Score: +0.5
    On the -2 to +2 scale, +0.5 reflects a mildly earnest choice: it improves clarity, but it also simplifies reality. The seven-tier policy scheme produces small groups and unstable distributions, so collapsing bins makes medians easier to compare and the policy gradient easier to read at a glance. This works well for persuasion because viewers can quickly parse the structure without getting lost in sparse categories. What works less well is that within-bin differences are hidden, and some nuance is lost. I considered keeping all seven tiers or using five tiers, but I settled on three because it balanced interpretability with enough policy resolution to support the proposition. 
    Restrictive = Most restrictive + Very restrictive + Restrictive
Middle      = Some restrictions/protections
Protective  = Protective + Very protective + Most protective
  • Leave out DC from the displacement figure to prevent it from dominating the scale. Score: +1
    On the -2 to +2 scale, +1 indicates mostly earnest intent with a real framing tradeoff. DC is not a state and is an extreme outlier on this metric, so excluding it prevents one jurisdiction from compressing the full distribution and obscuring state-to-state comparisons. This works well because the central pattern across states becomes visible instead of being visually anchored by one exceptional point. What did not work as well is the perception risk: removing a prominent protective case can look selective. I considered keeping DC with a log scale or adding an inset panel, but I settled on exclusion here with explicit disclosure to preserve readability.
  • Add a Kruskal-Wallis p-value annotation and a careful, non-causal subtitle. Score: +1.5
    On the -2 to +2 scale, +1.5 is strongly earnest because the annotation adds inferential support while the subtitle explicitly avoids causal overclaiming. The p-value was computed in MATLAB with pKW1 = kruskalwallis(y1, g1, "off"), where y1 is each state's out-of-state abortion percentage (excluding DC and missing values) and g1 is the three-level policy group. Kruskal-Wallis ranks all observations across groups, computes the H statistic from group rank sums (with tie correction), and converts H to a p-value using a chi-square reference distribution with k - 1 = 2 degrees of freedom. This helps persuasion by increasing credibility with skeptical readers who might otherwise dismiss the figure as purely descriptive. It worked well as a compact signal of statistical structure and careful interpretation. The downside is that p-values can be overweighted by readers and may distract from effect size; it also does not identify which specific pair of groups differs. I considered omitting inferential statistics or using only confidence intervals, but I chose this combination because it is concise and transparent about uncertainty.

AGAINST the Proposition: Restrictions may reduce abortions overall, not only reroute them.

Design decisions and rationale

  • Use the abortion rate by residence (per 1,000 women aged 15 to 44) instead of just the number of occurrences. Score: +2
    On the -2 to +2 scale, +2 is fully earnest because this directly matches the claim being tested: incidence among residents. It is persuasive for the counter-argument because it removes inflow bias from destination states and avoids confounding from population size. This worked well by making cross-state comparisons methodologically cleaner and harder to dismiss. A limitation is that residence-based rates still depend on reporting quality and do not capture every pathway. I considered occurrence-based counts and rates, but residence rate was the most defensible denominator for this question.
  • Group policy strength differently than For the Proposition. Score: -1.5
    Restrictive = Most restrictive + Very restrictive
Middle      = Restrictive + Some + Protective
Protective  = Very protective + Most protective
    On the -2 to +2 scale, -1.5 reflects a substantially persuasive and somewhat deceptive move because the asymmetric regrouping was chosen to sharpen endpoint contrast. The purpose of the second figure is to test whether extreme restriction corresponds to lower residence rates, and isolating only the strictest states makes that contrast visually stronger. This worked well for rhetorical clarity and made the endpoint comparison easier to see quickly. What did not work as well is comparability: using a different grouping logic than Figure 1 can look engineered for effect. I considered reusing the exact same bins as the pro figure or modeling policy as a continuous ordinal scale, but I chose this structure to stress-test the extreme-policy claim.
  • Force category order to start with Protective (leftmost) and end with Restrictive (rightmost). Score: +0.5
    On the -2 to +2 scale, +0.5 is mildly earnest: ordering improves readability but still nudges interpretation. Left-to-right from protective to restrictive creates a clear conceptual gradient and helps viewers compare endpoints immediately. This works well because directionality is understood quickly without changing any underlying values. The downside is subtle priming toward a narrative arc. I considered ordering by median (more rhetorically forceful) or alphabetically (more neutral but less meaningful), and I settled on conceptual order because it is transparent and defensible.
  • Add a national-average reference line. Score: -1
    On the -2 to +2 scale, -1 marks a moderately persuasive framing device. The national-average line gives a simple baseline, and here it works especially well rhetorically because the blue protective group sits above the line while the other groups sit below it. That split can almost trick readers into a simplified conclusion that "restrictive means lower" without fully engaging with overlap and within-group variation, which is exactly why it supports the against-the-proposition narrative so efficiently. What worked well is immediate interpretability; what worked less well is that a single baseline can oversimplify within-group spread. I considered confidence-interval bands or no baseline at all, but I kept the reference line because it makes the comparison legible in one glance.
  • Label a few lowest-rate states within the Restrictive group. Score: -0.5
    On the -2 to +2 scale, -0.5 is slightly deceptive because the labeling is selective but not fabricated. Highlighting a few low-rate restrictive states helps persuasion by creating memorable exemplars that reinforce the con narrative. This worked well for storytelling and audience recall. The weakness is fairness: unlabeled counterexamples remain visually present but less salient, which can feel like cherry-picking. I considered labeling median states, labeling both low and high outliers, or avoiding labels, and I chose low-only labels to emphasize the specific argument this panel is designed to make.

Final Reflection

One deliberate constraint I imposed was using boxplots for both figures. This functioned as a methodological control: the same summary statistic (median), the same distributional representation (IQR), and the same swarm overlay of individual states were used in each case. By holding the graphical form constant, I limited the extent to which visual encoding itself could bias interpretation. Using a mean-bar chart for one argument and a boxplot for the other would have introduced asymmetry in statistical emphasis. Standardizing the format ensured that differences in persuasion stemmed primarily from conceptual framing rather than graphical manipulation, which increased fairness across arguments and reduced overt deception. Across both visualizations, the most straightforward part of the process was selecting outcome measures that directly matched the mechanisms being tested: percent obtaining abortions out of state for displacement, and abortion rate by residence for incidence. A key lesson was that selecting different data views can legitimately produce different stories from the same policy landscape. That was my central goal in this assignment: to show how choosing displacement-focused data in one figure and incidence-focused data in the other changes the narrative emphasis without fabricating results. What proved more difficult, and more revealing, was recognizing how much interpretive weight rests on categorization, ordering, and framing rather than on the raw data themselves. I liked how modest structural changes, such as collapsing tiers differently or adding a reference line, could noticeably shift the strength of the narrative without altering any underlying values.

At the same time, the assignment made clear that persuasion often operates at the level of categorization and grouping. The most consequential choices were not color schemes or axis limits, but how policy tiers were collapsed and defined differently across the two figures. In the "for" figure, collapsing into three broad bins emphasized systemic polarization and stabilized medians. In the "against" figure, isolating the most extreme restrictive states sharpened contrast and amplified endpoint separation. Both approaches are defensible, yet the asymmetry illustrates how grouping logic can subtly privilege one interpretation over another. That decision was rhetorically powerful precisely because it reshaped the comparison structure. Also, excluding DC from one figure but not the other highlights a tension between interpretability and perceived neutrality. Removing outliers can improve clarity, yet it can also appear strategic. In this case, I attempted balance by retaining DC in the incidence analysis. The experience reinforced that transparency about exclusions is essential when visualizations support contested claims. Overall, this exercise showed me that responsible visualization requires disclosing not just methods, but also the framing choices that shape interpretation.
